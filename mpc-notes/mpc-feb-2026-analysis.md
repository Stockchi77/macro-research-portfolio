# Bank of England MPC Minutes Analysis — February 2026

**Meeting date:** 4 February 2026  
**Published:** 5 February 2026  
**Decision:** Bank Rate maintained at **3.75%** (5–4 vote)  
**Next meeting:** 19 March 2026

---

## Summary

The MPC held Bank Rate at 3.75% by a narrow 5–4 majority, with four members voting for an immediate 25bp cut to 3.50%. The vote split is notably more dovish than previous meetings and signals that the next decision will be a "closer call." The Committee acknowledged that disinflation is progressing, but members diverged on whether remaining inflationary pressures have receded sufficiently to justify faster easing.

---

## Inflation Outlook

- CPI fell from **3.8% in September 2025** to **3.4% in December 2025**
- CPI is projected to return to the **2% target from April 2026**, largely driven by energy price developments including measures from Budget 2025
- This near-term path was **notably lower** than the November MPR forecast
- Services price inflation and wage growth have continued to ease, but remain **above target-consistent levels**
- UK import prices are providing a modest disinflationary impulse, partly reflecting the drag from US tariffs on global export price growth

---

## Labour Market

- Unemployment rate has risen to **just over 5%**
- The labour market has continued to **loosen**, contributing to a slightly wider output gap versus the November forecast
- However, **vacancies have started to tick up**, suggesting the pace of loosening may be slowing
- Agents' pay survey showed pay settlements slowing to **3.4%** — close to target-consistent levels
- Weak productivity growth remains a concern, as it keeps **unit labour cost (ULC) growth elevated** even as headline wage growth moderates

---

## Output Gap & Activity

- Underlying GDP growth has remained **below estimates of potential**
- Agents' intelligence points to a **subdued demand outlook**
- The February Report incorporated a **slightly wider output gap** than November, reflecting both weaker activity and labour market slack
- Household saving rate remains elevated; risk that consumption recovery is slower than projected
- Bank lending and broad money growth have **edged higher**, suggesting monetary policy is not excessively restrictive

## Member Votes & Rationale

### Voted to Hold (5 members)

| Member | Key Reasoning |
|--------|--------------|
| **Andrew Bailey** | Risks more balanced; scope for easing but no commitment to timing |
| **Megan Greene** | Inflation persistence risk remains; forward indicators still elevated; prefers to wait for clearer evidence |
| **Clare Lombardelli** | Wages and underlying inflation still strong; uncertain how much slack is bearing down on inflation; cost of cutting too quickly outweighs cost of waiting |
| **Catherine L Mann** | Near-term disinflation partly mechanical; wage moderation in train but not complete; cutting now would overweight one-off factors |
| **Huw Pill** | Underlying disinflation intact but incomplete; concerned policy withdrawal since Aug 2024 has been overly rapid; favours cautious approach |

### Voted to Cut 25bp (4 members)

| Member | Key Reasoning |
|--------|--------------|
| **Sarah Breeden** | Upside risks diminished; new analysis supports faster normalisation; downside risks from saving rate and labour market warrant insurance cut |
| **Swati Dhingra** | Persistence scenario less compelling given weak activity; market-implied rate path too tight; downside policy error more costly |
| **Dave Ramsden** | Risks tilted to downside; confident wage growth will reach target-consistent levels this year; neutral rate ~3% implies policy still too restrictive |
| **Alan Taylor** | Cumulative forecast revisions point to lower inflation and more slack; inflation persistence scenario less likely; neutral rate of 3% should be in sight |

## Key Themes & Takeaways

### 1. Disinflation vs. Persistence
The central debate is whether the near-term fall in CPI (driven partly by energy and fiscal measures) will be sufficient to anchor expectations and normalise wage/price-setting — or whether underlying pressures will prove stickier. Hawks want more evidence; doves believe the process is sufficiently advanced.

### 2. The Neutral Rate Question
Several dovish members explicitly referenced a neutral rate of around **3%**, implying Bank Rate at 3.75% remains materially restrictive. Hawks are less convinced, partly due to uncertainty around where neutral actually lies.

### 3. Closer Call Ahead
The Committee explicitly flagged that "judgements around further policy easing will become a closer call." With a 5–4 split and two of the five hold voters (Bailey and Mann) expressing more dovish-leaning reasoning, the March meeting could plausibly deliver a cut.

### 4. Market Pricing vs. MPC Conditioning
The central projection was conditioned on a market curve implying Bank Rate settling around **3.75% in 2029**. The median Market Participants Survey respondent expected rates to eventually settle at **3.25%** — closer to the dovish members' implied neutral rate view.

## What to Watch Ahead of March Meeting

- **February CPI** (due mid-March) — key test of whether disinflation is on track
- **Labour market data** — unemployment, vacancies, and wage growth
- **Retail sales / consumption** — will the saving rate normalise?
- **Global developments** — US tariff policy and its pass-through to UK import prices
- **Inflation expectations surveys** — BoE/Ipsos, Citi/YouGov
